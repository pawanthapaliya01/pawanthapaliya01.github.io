About Me

Hi there! I'm Pawan Kumar Thapaliya, a PhD candidate in Applied Physics at the University of South Florida. My research sits at the intersection of computational biophysics and neurobiology, where I use mathematical modeling, machine learning, and signal processing to explore how brain cells, particularly astrocytes, communicate and respond during stressful conditions like ischemia and epilepsy.

Astrocytes are the most abundant cell type in the brain and are now recognized for their multifunctional roles beyond supporting neurons. These star-shaped cells help maintain brain function by regulating ion homeostasis, supporting neurons metabolically, and contributing to synaptic function. My research focuses on the dynamic interactions between astrocytes and neurons, using Markov models to simulate ion dynamics (sodium, calcium) and glutamate cycling during energy deprivation. I investigate how astrocytes adapt to metabolic stress and how these adaptations impact brain health, particularly in disorders like stroke, epilepsy, and Alzheimer's disease.

Astrocytes are also crucial in maintaining the structural integrity of the brain, including regulating the blood-brain barrier, and facilitating neuronal plasticity and synapse formation. Additionally, astrocytes participate in intracellular and intercellular signaling through calcium waves, which enable communication across vast areas of the brain. By incorporating these functionalities, my research aims to integrate computational models with experimental data to uncover the mechanisms behind neurovascular coupling, synaptic function, and neuroplasticity under metabolic stress.

By combining data-driven techniques like deep learning and traditional biophysical models, I aim to contribute to the development of therapeutic strategies for neurological disorders. My work continues to evolve, expanding our understanding of the intricate roles astrocytes play in brain health and disease.

When I'm not immersed in research or coding, I enjoy hiking, cooking, landscape photography, and reading books on science and history.
