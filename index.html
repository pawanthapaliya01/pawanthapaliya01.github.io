About Me

Hi there! I'm Pawan Kumar Thapaliya, a PhD candidate in Neuroscience and Computational Neurobiology at the University of South Florida. My research focuses on understanding how astrocytes — the brain's support cells — manage ionic balance, energy use, and signaling during stress, such as stroke and ischemia. Essentially, I combine biology and mathematics to uncover how sodium, calcium, and pH dynamics in astrocytes influence brain health and vulnerability.

I take an integrated computational and experimental approach. I develop biophysical models to simulate sodium (Naa), calcium, and ATP dynamics in astrocytes and then validate these predictions using brain slices and in vivo mouse models. My experiments include imaging ionic dynamics with ion and glutamate reporters, FRET-based ATP sensors, patch-clamp recordings, and ion-selective microelectrodes. This combination allows me to link cellular mechanisms to measurable outcomes.

Some key findings from my work include:

  • Regional sodium signaling: Neocortical astrocytes exhibit larger sodium transients than hippocampal astrocytes, driven by NMDA and AMPA receptor activity, leading to higher ATP consumption.
  • NBCe1 function: NBCe1 reduces acidosis during ischemia but increases sodium influx and ATP depletion, highlighting a protective-but-costly trade-off.
  • NCX1 roles: Neuronal NCX1 promotes calcium overload and glutamate release, whereas astrocytic NCX1 stabilizes ionic balance and protects tissue during spreading depolarizations.

Broadly, I'm interested in computational neuroscience, astrocytic physiology, and brain energy metabolism, particularly how computational models and experiments together can reveal mechanisms that may guide future therapeutic strategies for stroke and other neurological disorders.
